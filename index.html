I am a Ph.D candidate and NCI F99 Predoctoral Fellow in the Department of Biochemistry at the University of Utah

I work in the lab of Jared Rutter @ the University of Utah. My primary focuses are developing computational tools and mathematical methods to aid in metabolism research and applying these and other methods to obtain novel biological insights into complex systems. Recently, I have been using these methods to explore the role of metabolism in cancer progression and heart failure. I also develop the bioinformatics software suites, Metaboverse, which aids researchers in contextualizing their metabolic data and automates the process of identifying regulatory events within their data; and XPRESSyourself, which adds to and automates the quantification and analysis of ribosome profiling and other gene expression data. My work is currently funded by a NCI F99 Predoctoral Fellowship (1F99CA253744).

Previously, I worked in the lab of Julianne Grose as an undergraduate isolating and phenotypically and genomically characterizing novel Erwinia and Brevibacillus bacteriophages.

You can contact me at jordan.berg[at]biochem.utah.edu

Metaboverse: Metabolic network pattern recognition tools contextualize multi-omic data and reveal disease-relevant signatures

With Youjia Zhou, Yeyun Ouyang, T. Cameron Waller, Sara Nowinski, Tyler Van Ry, Ian George, James Cox, Bei Wang, and Jared Rutter

  Status: Working Papers

Metaboverse: Metabolic network pattern recognition tools contextualize multi-omic data and reveal disease-relevant signatures

Abstract

Metabolism is a complex biological system, with variable inputs, outputs, and modifiers. The harmony between these factors consequently determines the health and stability of a cell or an organism. Perturbations to any reaction can have rippling downstream effects, which can be challenging to trace across the total reaction network, particularly when the effects occur between complex interconnected pathways. This problem is further confounded by the inherent limitations of current metabolomics approaches that garner incomplete datasets within this network. Researchers have primarily utilized reductionist approaches to understand these complicated metabolic reaction systems; however, these methods often limit the scope of the analysis. Systems-centric omics approaches can also be limited when only a handful of high magnitude signals in the data are prioritized for interpretation. To address these challenges, we developed Metaboverse, a user-friendly application and introduce algorithms that allow for the rapid and thorough extraction of all regulatory patterns and trends within user data that is overlaid on metabolic and other reaction networks. We demonstrate the ability of Metaboverse to rapidly extract and distil reliable patterns from multi-omics datasets and demonstrate its ability to reveal novel contextual patterns. Moreover, using Metaboverse, we identify a putative mechanism by which mitochondrial fatty acid synthesis-impaired yeast respond to decreased citrate levels, a metabolite essential to respiratory growth. We also identify a putative metabolic regulatory signature in lung adenocarcinomas with implications for patient survival. This framework will be foundational in increasing our ability to holistically interpret and understand metabolic events, perturbations, and gene-metabolite intra-cooperativity. Metaboverse is freely available under a GPL-3.0 license at https://github.com/Metaboverse/.

XPRESSyourself: Enhancing, Standardizing, and Automating Ribosome Profiling Computational Analyses Yields Improved Insight into Data

With Jonathan Belyeu, Jeffrey Morgan, Yeyun Ouyang, Alex Bott, Aaron Quinlan, Jason Gertz, and Jared Rutter

  Status: Published Work

XPRESSyourself: Enhancing, Standardizing, and Automating Ribosome Profiling Computational Analyses Yields Improved Insight into Data

Abstract

Ribosome profiling, an application of nucleic acid sequencing for monitoring ribosome activity, has revolutionized our understanding of protein translation dynamics. This technique has been available for a decade, yet the current state and standardization of publicly available computational tools for these data is bleak. We introduce XPRESSyourself, an analytical toolkit that eliminates barriers and bottlenecks associated with this specialized data type by filling gaps in the computational toolset for both experts and non-experts of ribosome profiling. XPRESSyourself automates and standardizes analysis procedures, decreasing time-to-discovery and increasing reproducibility. This toolkit acts as a reference implementation of current best practices in ribosome profiling analysis. We demonstrate this toolkit’s performance on publicly available ribosome profiling data by rapidly identifying hypothetical mechanisms related to neurodegenerative phenotypes and neuroprotective mechanisms of the small-molecule ISRIB during acute cellular stress. XPRESSyourself brings robust, rapid analysis of ribosome-profiling data to a broad and ever-expanding audience and will lead to more reproducible and accessible measurements of translation regulation. XPRESSyourself software is perpetually open-source under the GPL-3.0 license and is hosted at https://github.com/XPRESSyourself/, where users can access additional documentation and report software issues.

Interactive Article Exploration: "Previously undetected super-spreading of Mycobacterium tuberculosis revealed by deep sequencing

A re-visualization of infection spread in a tuberculosis community outbreak.

14 Feb 2020

Interactive Article Exploration: "Previously undetected super-spreading of Mycobacterium tuberculosis revealed by deep sequencing

* Find the original paper here [1].

* Find the code here.

Earlier this month, a paper was published in eLife detailing the deep sequencing of several tuberculosis patients from an outbreak within a Canadian Inuit community. By performing heirarchal clustering of the SNP data, the authors were able to determine the presence of a "super-spreader" during the outbreak. Reading the paper, I became interested in trying to create an interactive visualization of the data that accounted for sequence similarity of the tuberculosis strains isolated from the patients, as well as time derived from the month and year of diagnosis.

In order to map spread, I took the CI 95% SNP data and created a square-form similarity matrix by using a Hamming distance calculation [2]. I then generated a two-dimensional network, where nodes are connected by time and SNP similarity. To attempt to model the spread of the disease, I traversed time and found each patients most similar SNP neighbors, assuming that each patient was infected when diagnosed, and that SNP variation indicates progression of the disease. Progression using this paradigm seemed to be most stable when for each iteration, I found the 3 minimum distance scores and linked any patient with those distance values compared to the current patient of interest. Once a patient had been connected, they were removed from further analysis. Again, this probably over-simplifies the problem, but an interesting approach nonetheless (hopefully).

Disclaimer: It is interesting that while super-spreaders are identified, they are different than those identified in the original study. However, this was mostly a learning exercise for visualization of temporal data. As compelling as this view of the data might be, it is preliminary and probably riddled with errors. Take this with a grain of salt...

In order to interact with the graph, you can click a patient circle and drag to reposition the graph. To pan, click and drag the background. To zoom in and out, use the scroll wheel. To move through time, drag the ball on the timeline below the graph, or press play or pause. Red coloring of the patient circle indicates time of diagnosis. As you can see, there is a trend/correlation with SNP similarity and time of diagnosis. Also, the reference genome was excluded from the graph. Note: The two outlier strains, as identified in the original study, usually appear by themselves on the lower right corner of the graph, so you may need to pan over to view those samples.
