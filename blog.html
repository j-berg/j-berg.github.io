Interactive Article Exploration: "Previously undetected super-spreading of Mycobacterium tuberculosis revealed by deep sequencing

A re-visualization of infection spread in a tuberculosis community outbreak.

14 Feb 2020

Interactive Article Exploration: "Previously undetected super-spreading of Mycobacterium tuberculosis revealed by deep sequencing

* Find the original paper here [1].

* Find the code here.

Earlier this month, a paper was published in eLife detailing the deep sequencing of several tuberculosis patients from an outbreak within a Canadian Inuit community. By performing heirarchal clustering of the SNP data, the authors were able to determine the presence of a "super-spreader" during the outbreak. Reading the paper, I became interested in trying to create an interactive visualization of the data that accounted for sequence similarity of the tuberculosis strains isolated from the patients, as well as time derived from the month and year of diagnosis.

In order to map spread, I took the CI 95% SNP data and created a square-form similarity matrix by using a Hamming distance calculation [2]. I then generated a two-dimensional network, where nodes are connected by time and SNP similarity. To attempt to model the spread of the disease, I traversed time and found each patients most similar SNP neighbors, assuming that each patient was infected when diagnosed, and that SNP variation indicates progression of the disease. Progression using this paradigm seemed to be most stable when for each iteration, I found the 3 minimum distance scores and linked any patient with those distance values compared to the current patient of interest. Once a patient had been connected, they were removed from further analysis. Again, this probably over-simplifies the problem, but an interesting approach nonetheless (hopefully).

Disclaimer: It is interesting that while super-spreaders are identified, they are different than those identified in the original study. However, this was mostly a learning exercise for visualization of temporal data. As compelling as this view of the data might be, it is preliminary and probably riddled with errors. Take this with a grain of salt...

In order to interact with the graph, you can click a patient circle and drag to reposition the graph. To pan, click and drag the background. To zoom in and out, use the scroll wheel. To move through time, drag the ball on the timeline below the graph, or press play or pause. Red coloring of the patient circle indicates time of diagnosis. As you can see, there is a trend/correlation with SNP similarity and time of diagnosis. Also, the reference genome was excluded from the graph. Note: The two outlier strains, as identified in the original study, usually appear by themselves on the lower right corner of the graph, so you may need to pan over to view those samples.

Introducing: XPRESSyourself -- an automated pipeline for ribosome profiling analysis

A discussion of the software package XPRESSyourself, which introduces new tools for ribosome profiling and acts as a reference pipeline for ribosome profiling data analysis.

19 Jan 2020

Introducing: XPRESSyourself -- an automated pipeline for ribosome profiling analysis

* Find the software here.

* Find the publication here [1].

When I began graduate school at the University of Utah, and began considering working in the Rutter lab, Jared proposed a project to me which I found fascinating. He was interested in looking at protein translation using ribosome profilng [2], but in the context of metabolism of interest to the Rutter lab. I was hooked by the idea as it would allow a nice blend of wet lab and dry lab work. However, as I began exploring the data, I quickly found that the analysis side of things still had a lot of room for development, and there was no consensus dry methodology, and I realized that everyone designing their own scripts for processing these data could be problematic as much as it was inefficient. Additionally, key steps of data processing were missing from the open source community. Soon enough, I began thinking of ways I could help solve these issues and began work on XPRESSyourself.


What does XPRESSyourself do?

XPRESSyourself's goals are three-fold:

1) Act as a reference implementation of ribosome profiling data analysis.
2) Provide automated tools missing from the ribosome profiling toolkit.
3) Where tools exist, help automate the tasks into a well-integrated pipeline.

XPRESSyourself is designed to require minimal inputs from the user and guide the user through the proper set-up of experimental variables to pass into the packages XPRESSyourself utilizes. XPRESSyourself is split into two packages: XPRESSpipe and XPRESSplot. The former handles the processing and quality control of the raw FASTQ sequence files, and the latter handles normalization and analysis of the processed data. While the input requirements are detailed in the documentation and help menus, we tried to make this tool as approachable as possible. Therefore, we designed a sort of "choose your own adventure" style command builder (shown below) to walk the user through every consideration they would need to make for running XPRESSpipe, and Jupyter Notebook for example analyses for XPRESSplot. By centralizing and automating the code needed to process this specialized data type, hopefully, reproducibility will increase and we will be able to extract the maximum amount of data from these labor-intensive and expensive experiments.

View of the XPRESSpipe command builder.


Adding to the toolkit

As mentioned earlier, when I began analyzing my own ribosome profiling data, I began to discover some key tools were missing from the toolkit. The most glaring vacancy in the toolkit centered around removing ribosome profiling-specific biases from the data. As the kinetics of translation initiation and termination are much slower than elongation dynamics [3], which is frequently the of most common interest to users of this technology, initation and termination can often provide a more murky picture of translation dynamics. This effect is further exacerbated by the use of cycloheximide during ribosome footprint harvesting. It is therefore recommended to disregard the first 45 nt and last 15 nt of each transcript, which are suspect to these kinetic consequences [4]. However, no open source tools exist for handling this problem. We decided to handle this issue during quantification of reads using a modified GTF reference file. However, as many transcripts in these records contain short exon 1 sections, often removing the first exon alone is not enough, and uneven truncation can have problematic consequences on quantification between transcripts (see image below). Therefore, we handle this truncation recursively.

The consequences of improper trimming.

Other features act to thread together steps of quality control that previously were not fully automated. Metagene read density, intron-specific gene-specific read coverage, read periodicity, and codon distributions are processed for each sample and a publication quality summary PDF is output so the user can quickly view and compare all samples to assess the quality of their ribosome profiling libraries.

We designed XPRESSyourself to be as flexible as possible. Therefore, in addition to being able to processing ribosome profiling data (and its paired RNA-seq data for translation efficiency calculations), we have used it to analyze single-end RNA-seq, paired-end RNA-seq (we show an example in the supplement where we processed TCGA data), and more sequencing platforms. We have also used XPRESSplot to analyze even proteomics data.


Conclusions

Certainly this is only meant to be a quick overview of the capabilities of XPRESSyourself. If you are interested in learning more about all the capabilities XPRESSyourself has to offer, I would suggest starting with the paper and then explore of the documentation if the tool looks useful to you. If you run into any challenges while using the software, feel free to reach out to me by submitting an issue on Github. I hope you find this tool useful! Happy analyzing!

Understanding scaling in heatmaps

A brief summary of the utility of plotting heatmaps using z-scored gene expression values in pattern identification.

09 Apr 2019

Understanding scaling in heatmaps

Heatmaps are an effective method for representing large swaths of data at once and uncovering underlying patterns within the data. Probably the most common use in biology is within high-throughput genomics experiments, such as RNA-seq. In RNA-seq experiments, one may want to uncover molecular patterns characteristic of a particular biological background, or track changes in gene expression across time. Whenever I make a heatmap, I always get the same question: Why are you using Z-scores for the gene expression data? Why not use fold changes? This blog post is intended as a blog post to summarize my answers to some common questions. I will be using examples relevant to RNA-seq, but the principles are applicable to other data types.

In the following examples, I will be borrowing heavily from a fantastic blog post by BIOMICKWATSON, titled "You probably don't understand heatmaps". The purpose of my blog post is not to steal the thunder of BIOMICKWATSON, but rather to offer a summary of questions I have encountered and an easy place to access this information (rather than me trying to explain myself over and over again).


Why Z-score?

Z-scoring (or standard scoring) of data essentially boils down to going gene by, calculating the mean of the the values across all samples for the gene, then determining how many standard deviations the actual value is from the mean. This is also commonly referred to as "mean centering." This is done using the following formula:

$$ {z = (x – μ) / σ} $$

Let's break this down a little:
- x: A given gene expression measurement (ex: 40)
- μ: The mean of gene expression values across samples for a given gene (ex: 25)
- σ: The standard deviation across samples for a given gene (ex: 5)
- z: The outcoming z-score for each gene expression measurement

So in this example, the output z-score for this particular gene in this sample would be:

$$ {3 = (40 – 25) / 5} $$

So this gene having a z = 3 means its expression is 3 standard deviations above the mean. Pretty impressive, right?

Scoring values along a normal distribution. Source: Wikipedia


But Z-scores make no sense! Why not log2(fold change)?

I get this response a lot, and I get it, Z-scoring gene expression may not be super intuitive. And I will admit, while Z-scores give you significance and direction, using solely Z-score misses an important piece of information: magnitude. But to plot a heatmap using using log2(fold change) also misses significance!

In cases where magnitude of change is important, I recommend people use a volcano plot to visualize their data. This captures magnitude, direction, and statistical significance, but will lose information like population variability (maybe have your experimental samples have one signature and the other half have another). You also cannot plot expression over a time course. But let's talk more about why Z-scores should be used in heatmaps, that's why we're here after all!
